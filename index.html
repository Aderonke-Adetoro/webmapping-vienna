Color Accessibility Study

Seeing the Lines

How does color vision deficiency affect the readability of Vienna's U-Bahn map? This interactive project simulates protanopia, deuteranopia, and tritanopia on a real map of the five U-Bahn lines — revealing which color pairs become indistinguishable and what that means for navigation.

Overview

What This Project Shows

🔬

CVD Simulation

Using the Machado et al. (2009) physiologically-based model, we apply SVG <feColorMatrix> filters to transform the entire map canvas — simulating how a person with color blindness sees the network.

📊

Contrast Analysis

The WCAG 2.2 contrast ratio is computed for every pair of U-Bahn line colors under each simulation. This quantifies which color pairs fail accessibility thresholds and by how much.

🎚️

Adjustable Severity

CVD exists on a spectrum. A severity slider interpolates between normal vision and full cone absence, letting you explore how partial deficiency affects line distinguishability.

The Network

Vienna's Five U-Bahn Lines

Vienna's U-Bahn (underground railway) consists of five lines operated by Wiener Linien. Each line is identified by a distinct color — a design choice that works well for most people, but creates challenges for the approximately 8% of males and 0.5% of females with some form of color vision deficiency.

U1

Oberlaa – Leopoldau. Runs north–south through the city center.

U2

Schottentor – Seestadt. Serves the northeast and Donaustadt.

U3

Ottakring – Simmering. Crosses the city east–west through Stephansplatz.

U4

Hütteldorf – Heiligenstadt. Follows the Wien River and the Danube Canal.

U6

Siebenhirten – Floridsdorf. The longest line, running along the Gürtel.
