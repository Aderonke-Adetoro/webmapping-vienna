Background

Understanding Color Vision Deficiency

Color vision deficiency (CVD), commonly called "color blindness," is a condition in which the eye's cone photoreceptors do not respond to light wavelengths as expected. It is not a form of blindness — people with CVD see a full range of brightness — but certain colors that look distinct to a typical viewer may appear similar or identical to someone with CVD.

How Human Color Vision Works

The human retina contains three types of cone photoreceptors, each sensitive to a different range of wavelengths:

  • L-cones (Long-wavelength) — peak sensitivity around 564 nm, responding most strongly to red–yellow light.
  • M-cones (Medium-wavelength) — peak sensitivity around 534 nm, responding most strongly to green–yellow light.
  • S-cones (Short-wavelength) — peak sensitivity around 420 nm, responding most strongly to blue–violet light.

The brain interprets color by comparing the signals from all three cone types. A person with typical trichromatic vision has all three cone types functioning normally. When one cone type is absent or has an altered spectral response, the brain receives a reduced set of signals, resulting in a compressed or shifted color perception.

Types of Color Vision Deficiency

CVD is classified by which cone type is affected and whether that cone is absent (suffix -anopia) or has a shifted spectral sensitivity (suffix -anomaly). This project simulates the three complete-absence forms:

Protanopia (L-cone absent)

The long-wavelength cone is missing entirely. Red light produces a much weaker signal, causing reds to appear dark and muddy, and red–green distinctions to collapse. Protanopia affects approximately 1.0% of males.

On the U-Bahn map, protanopia makes it particularly difficult to distinguish U1 (red) from U4 (green), and U3 (orange) from U4 (green), since all three appear as similar yellowish-brown tones.

Deuteranopia (M-cone absent)

The medium-wavelength cone is missing. Like protanopia, this causes red–green confusion, but with a different pattern because the M-cone response curve differs from the L-cone. Deuteranopia is the most common complete-absence form, affecting approximately 1.2% of males.

On the U-Bahn map, deuteranopia creates confusion between U1 (red), U3 (orange), U4 (green), and U6 (brown), as these all shift towards similar ochre and khaki tones.

Tritanopia (S-cone absent)

The short-wavelength cone is missing. This is the rarest form, affecting fewer than 0.01% of the population regardless of sex (tritanopia is autosomal, not X-linked). It causes blue–yellow confusion rather than red–green confusion.

On the U-Bahn map, tritanopia has less impact on line distinguishability because the U-Bahn colors are mostly in the red–green–orange range. However, the purple of U2 shifts towards a pinkish-red, potentially creating confusion with U1 (red).

Prevalence

CVD is far more common than most people realise. Because the most prevalent forms (protan and deutan) are X-linked recessive traits, they disproportionately affect males, who have only one X chromosome and therefore no backup copy of the gene.

  • Approximately 8% of males and 0.5% of females of Northern European descent have some form of red–green CVD.
  • In Vienna (population ~2 million), this means roughly 80,000 male residents may experience some degree of red–green color confusion when reading transit maps.
  • Tritanopia (blue–yellow) is autosomal and equally rare in both sexes, affecting fewer than 1 in 10,000 people.

Why This Matters for Cartography

Transit maps are among the most widely used cartographic products in daily life. Millions of people rely on them for wayfinding, and color is the primary visual variable used to distinguish lines. When a map's color scheme fails for a significant portion of the population, it creates a real accessibility barrier — not an abstract design concern.

Good cartographic practice requires considering color accessibility at the design stage, not as an afterthought. This means:

  • Choosing color palettes that maintain sufficient contrast under the most common CVD types (protan and deutan).
  • Providing redundant visual encoding — such as line patterns, labels, or shape markers — so that color is not the sole distinguishing variable.
  • Testing designs with simulation tools (like this project) to identify problem areas before publication.

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.2, specifically Success Criterion 1.4.11 ("Non-text Contrast"), requires a minimum contrast ratio of 3:1 between graphical elements that convey information. Our interactive map computes this ratio for every pair of U-Bahn line colors under each CVD simulation, making the accessibility impact measurable rather than subjective.

The Simulation Model: Machado et al. (2009)

This project uses the color simulation matrices published by Machado, Oliveira, and Fernandes in their 2009 paper "A Physiologically-Based Model for Simulation of Color Vision Deficiency" (IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics, 15(6), 1291–1298).

Unlike earlier simulation methods that used simplified heuristic transformations, the Machado model is grounded in the physiology of the human visual system. It models the spectral absorption characteristics of each cone type and derives 3×3 transformation matrices that convert an input RGB color to the color a person with a specific CVD type would perceive.

How the Matrices Work

Each CVD type is represented by a 3×3 matrix. For a given input color [R, G, B], the simulated color is computed as:

[R', G', B'] = M × [R, G, B]T

where M is the CVD-specific matrix. Because the transformation is a linear matrix multiplication in RGB space, it can be applied efficiently as an SVG <feColorMatrix> filter — which is exactly what our interactive map does.

Severity Interpolation

CVD exists on a spectrum. Some people have a complete absence of a cone type (e.g. protanopia), while others have an altered but still functional cone (e.g. protanomaly). The severity slider in our simulation interpolates between the identity matrix (normal vision) and the full CVD matrix:

M(t) = (1 − t) × I + t × MCVD

where t ranges from 0 (normal vision) to 1 (full dichromacy). This linear interpolation is an approximation — the actual perceptual change is nonlinear — but it provides a useful interactive tool for exploring how increasing deficiency severity progressively reduces color discrimination.
